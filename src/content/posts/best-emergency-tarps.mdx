# Best Emergency Tarps and Shelter for Survival

Shelter is one of the survival priorities—exposure kills faster than dehydration or starvation. A quality tarp weighs ounces, packs small, and provides protection from rain, wind, sun, and ground moisture.

This guide covers the best tarps and emergency shelters for bug out bags and survival kits.

## Quick Picks

|------|----------------|--------|
| Budget Emergency | SOL Emergency Bivvy | 3.8 oz |
| Best Tarp Overall | Aqua Quest Defender | 26 oz |
| Ultralight | Sea to Summit Ultra-Sil | 11 oz |
| Heavy Duty | Kelty Noah's Tarp | 28 oz |

## Tarp vs. Tent vs. Bivvy

Each shelter type has its place:

| Shelter | Weight | Setup | Protection | Best For |
|---------|--------|-------|------------|----------|
| Tarp | 10-30 oz | Medium | Good | Versatile, lightweight |
| Tent | 2-6 lbs | Slow | Excellent | Base camp, insects |
| Bivvy | 3-8 oz | Fast | Basic | Emergency, ultralight |
| Hammock | 1-2 lbs | Medium | Good | Comfort, wet ground |

<Callout type="tip">
For bug out bags, a tarp + bivvy combination provides excellent protection at minimal weight. Use the bivvy for warmth and ground protection, with the tarp overhead for rain.
</Callout>

## Tarp Setup Configurations

Learn these basic setups before you need them:

### A-Frame (Most Common)

- Ridgeline between two trees
- Tarp draped over ridgeline
- Stakes or rocks hold corners
- **Pro:** Excellent rain runoff, stable
- **Con:** Limited headroom

### Lean-To

- One edge high (attached to tree/ridgeline)
- Opposite edge staked low
- **Pro:** Maximum open-side coverage, fire reflector
- **Con:** Less rain protection, wind exposed

### Flying Diamond

- Center point high (hiking pole or branch)
- Four corners staked low
- **Pro:** Good coverage, wind resistant
- **Con:** Requires center support

### Plow Point

- One corner pulled up high
- Opposite edge staked to ground
- **Pro:** Wind sheds, low footprint
- **Con:** Limited space

<Callout type="info">
**Practice at home first.** Setting up a tarp in the dark, in the rain, when exhausted is very different from a sunny backyard setup. Practice until you can do it blindfolded.
</Callout>

**Budget tip:** Use rocks and sticks instead of stakes when possible. A tarp with many tie-out points gives you more options.

## Ground Protection Strategy

A tarp keeps rain off, but you also need ground insulation:

1. **Emergency bivvy** — Reflective, waterproof, minimal weight
2. **Ground sheet/footprint** — Tyvek or polycryo, 1-4 oz
3. **Natural materials** — Leaves, pine needles, grass (primitive)
4. **Sleeping pad** — Foam or inflatable for insulation

<Callout type="warning">
Ground steals more body heat than air. Even with a great overhead tarp, sleeping directly on cold ground can lead to hypothermia. Always insulate below.
</Callout>

## Tarp Material Guide

| Material | Pros | Cons |
|----------|------|------|
| Silnylon | Light, strong, packs small | Stretches when wet |
| Polyethylene | Cheap, waterproof | Heavy, tears easily |
| Dyneema | Lightest, doesn't stretch | Very expensive |
| Ripstop Nylon (PU) | Durable, affordable | Heavier than silnylon |
| Canvas | Breathes, very durable | Heavy, can mold |

**For survival:** Silnylon or PU-coated ripstop nylon offer the best balance of weight, durability, and cost.
