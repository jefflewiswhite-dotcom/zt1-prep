Emergency Bivvy vs Sleeping Bag: Which Should You Pack?

Your sleep system is critical survival gear—exposure kills more people in emergencies than almost anything else. But weight matters in a bug out bag, so choosing the right shelter solution requires balancing warmth, weight, and packability.

Let’s break down when to choose an emergency bivvy versus a sleeping bag.

Quick Answer

Choose an emergency bivvy if: Weight is critical, you need maximum packability, or you’re building a get home bag or ultralight BOB.

Choose a sleeping bag if: You prioritize comfort, expect colder temperatures, or have a vehicle-based kit where weight matters less.

Best of both worlds: Pack an emergency bivvy as a backup/supplement to a lightweight sleeping bag.

The Best of Both Worlds

Many experienced preppers carry both:

  1. Lightweight sleeping bag (30-40°F rated, ~2 lbs) as primary
  2. Emergency bivvy as waterproof outer layer and backup

Using a bivvy over a sleeping bag:

  • Adds waterproof protection
  • Increases warmth by reflecting additional heat
  • Provides redundancy if one fails

This combo adds only 4-8 oz to your sleeping bag weight while significantly increasing your temperature range and redundancy.

Key insight: Bivvies don’t generate heat—they reflect your body heat. If you’re already hypothermic, a bivvy won’t save you. If you’re generating heat, it helps retain it.

Our Recommendations

For Get Home Bags

Get an emergency bivvy. Weight and packability trump comfort for a kit designed to get you home in 12-24 hours.

Our pick: SOL Emergency Bivvy (~$25)

For Bug Out Bags (Moderate Climates)

Get both. A lightweight sleeping bag (30-40°F) plus an emergency bivvy gives you comfort, warmth range, and redundancy for around 2.5 lbs total.

For Bug Out Bags (Cold Climates)

Prioritize a quality sleeping bag. A bivvy alone won’t keep you alive in genuinely cold conditions. Get a bag rated 10-20°F below the coldest temps you expect.

For Car Kits

Get a sleeping bag. You have the space, and waiting for help in a cold vehicle is much more bearable with a real sleeping bag.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can an emergency bivvy keep you warm enough to survive?
In mild conditions (50°F+), yes. In colder conditions, it can mean the difference between dangerous hypothermia and survivable cold, but it won't keep you comfortable. Below freezing, a bivvy alone is inadequate for most people.
How many times can you reuse an emergency bivvy?
Quality bivvies like the SOL Emergency Bivvy can be reused 3-10+ times with careful use. Emergency blankets (the thin crinkly kind) are typically single-use.
Does condensation inside a bivvy make you colder?
It can. Moisture conducts heat away from your body faster than dry air. Opening the bivvy periodically to vent moisture helps, as does wearing moisture-wicking base layers.
What's the best ultralight sleeping bag for a BOB?
Budget options include the TETON Sports bags. Ultralight backpacking bags (like Enlightened Equipment quilts) offer better weight-to-warmth ratios but cost $200+. For most BOB uses, a budget bag plus bivvy is adequate.
Should I pack an emergency blanket too?
If you have a bivvy, you don't need a blanket (bivvies are better). An emergency blanket as additional backup weighs almost nothing, so it doesn't hurt—but the bivvy is your primary.
